Six sleep trackers. Ninety nights of testing. Here is what the data actually showed.

The market for sleep tracking has matured significantly. In 2022, consumer-grade devices were barely more accurate than questionnaires. By 2026, the best of them are accurate enough to make real decisions from — but only two are genuinely worth buying for most people.

## The two worth buying

**Oura Ring Gen 4 — best for most people**

The Oura Ring is the benchmark for passive sleep tracking. You put it on, forget about it, and wake up to a detailed breakdown of sleep stages, HRV, respiratory rate, and body temperature deviation. No charger panic at 11pm. No deciding whether to sleep with something on your wrist.

Battery life is 8 days. The ring form factor means you don't notice it during sleep. The Gen 4 added improved HRV sensors and daytime activity detection — both meaningfully better than Gen 3.

The app is the best in the category. The readiness score correlates well with how you actually feel — once the algorithm has 2–3 weeks of baseline data. The first week is noisy.

Price: $349 one-time. No subscription required for most features (Oura Plus subscription adds AI insights at $5.99/month — optional).

**Whoop 4.0 — best for people who train hard**

Whoop is built around strain and recovery, not just sleep. If you train 4+ days a week, the daily strain score tells you when to push and when to back off in a way that Oura doesn't match.

The sleep coaching feature — which recommends a bedtime based on your planned wake time and recovery debt — is genuinely useful. The HRV measurement is reliable.

The subscription model ($239/year, hardware included) works if you actually engage with the platform. If you want to check your sleep data and forget about it, the ongoing cost isn't justified.

## The ones we'd skip

**Garmin Venu 3** — Excellent GPS and training data. Sleep accuracy is solid. But at $449, it's overpriced relative to Oura for sleep-focused buyers. Buy it if you run or cycle and want GPS — not as a pure sleep tracker.

**RingConn Gen 2** — The best budget option. HRV tracking is accurate. Sleep staging is more basic than Oura. If $349 is too much, this is the honest alternative.

**Fitbit Charge 6** — Google's integration has improved the platform. Sleep staging is acceptable. But Fitbit has been deprioritised since the Google acquisition and the long-term roadmap is unclear.

**Apple Watch Series 9** — The worst sleep tracker in this comparison. An 18-hour battery means you're either charging it overnight or wearing a half-charged device during the day. Sleep data quality is below every dedicated tracker here.

## What to look for

Three things determine whether a sleep tracker is worth buying:

1. **Battery life of at least 5 days** — anything less and you'll start skipping nights to charge it
2. **HRV measurement, not just heart rate** — HRV is the signal that tells you whether last night's sleep actually restored you
3. **A usable app** — raw data without context is noise

## Our recommendation

For 80% of readers: buy the **Oura Ring Gen 4**. It is the most passive, most comfortable, and most accurately validated consumer sleep tracker available.

If you train seriously and want daily recovery guidance integrated with training load: **Whoop 4.0**.

Everything else is a compromise.
