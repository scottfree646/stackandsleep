The Oura Ring Gen 4 is the best consumer sleep tracker available. After six months of daily wear, that conclusion is straightforward — but it comes with important caveats about what it actually does well.

## What it measures

The ring has five sensors on the inner surface:

- **Infrared PPG sensors** (3) — measures heart rate and HRV throughout the night
- **Red LED sensor** — measures blood oxygen (SpO2) for sleep-disordered breathing detection
- **NTC temperature sensor** — skin temperature, updated every minute overnight

From these five sensors, Oura's algorithm derives: sleep stages (light, deep, REM), HRV, resting heart rate, respiratory rate, and body temperature deviation from your personal baseline.

## Sleep stage accuracy

The most common question: is it accurate?

For total sleep time and sleep efficiency, Oura Gen 4 performs within ±15 minutes of PSG (polysomnography) in published studies. That's accurate enough to be useful.

Sleep stage accuracy is more nuanced. REM detection is reliable — Oura identifies REM with roughly 75–80% accuracy in published comparisons. Deep sleep is harder; the algorithm sometimes conflates light NREM with deep, particularly in people whose slow-wave sleep is less pronounced. If your app says you got 45 minutes of deep sleep but you feel terrible, trust how you feel.

HRV measurement (using rMSSD methodology) is consistently validated against ECG in studies. This is the most reliable metric the ring produces.

## The temperature deviation feature

This is Oura's most underrated feature. The ring tracks your skin temperature every minute overnight and shows you deviation from your personal baseline. A consistent elevation of +0.5–1.0°C in the 2–3 nights before you feel ill is detectable in hindsight — and increasingly, people report catching it in real time.

The feature also detects menstrual cycle phase in female users, which Oura has validated in separate research.

## What the app gets right

The readiness score — a 0–100 composite of HRV, resting heart rate, body temperature, sleep timing, and previous night's sleep — is more useful than any individual metric. It's a reasonable daily answer to the question: "Is today a day to push, or a day to recover?"

The trend charts across 30/90 days are where Oura's value compounds. Seeing that your HRV drops every time you drink alcohol, even one drink, is more persuasive than any article about alcohol and sleep.

## What it doesn't do well

**Daytime activity tracking is mediocre.** Step counts are inconsistent. The ring doesn't track workouts well without phone GPS. If activity tracking matters to you, Garmin does it better.

**The app sends too many notifications.** Turn off most of them. The data is there when you want it; you don't need a push notification every time your sleep score updates.

**The first 2–3 weeks are noisy.** The algorithm needs time to establish your personal baseline. Don't make conclusions in week one.

## Who it's for

Buy the Oura Ring Gen 4 if:
- You want passive, comfortable sleep tracking without a wrist device
- HRV trends and readiness scores are your primary interest
- You don't need GPS or fitness tracking from the same device

Skip it if:
- You train seriously and want daily strain/load tracking — Whoop is better for this
- You're already satisfied with a Garmin with HRV Status enabled
- You're not willing to pay $349 upfront — consider RingConn Gen 2 at $279

## The verdict

The Oura Ring Gen 4 does what it promises: gives you reliable, passive sleep and recovery data that changes how you understand your own patterns over time. Six months in, it remains the first thing I check in the morning. That says something.
