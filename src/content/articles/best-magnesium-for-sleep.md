Magnesium is one of the few sleep supplements where the evidence is genuinely strong. But most people buy the wrong form — or the wrong dose — and notice nothing. Here's what you need to know.

## Why magnesium affects sleep

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body. For sleep, the key mechanisms are:

1. **GABA receptor binding** — Magnesium binds to GABA-A receptors, promoting the inhibitory nervous system activity that underlies sleep onset. Low magnesium is associated with hyperexcitability of the nervous system — the "wired but tired" state.

2. **NMDA receptor inhibition** — Magnesium blocks NMDA receptors involved in cortisol and stress signalling. This is why low magnesium correlates with elevated nighttime cortisol in some studies.

3. **Melatonin regulation** — Magnesium is a cofactor in the enzymatic conversion of tryptophan to melatonin. Deficiency can blunt melatonin production.

Importantly: these effects are most pronounced in people who are magnesium-deficient. If you already have adequate intracellular magnesium, supplementation has smaller (but still measurable) effects on sleep.

## The forms that work

### Magnesium Glycinate — the best choice for sleep

Magnesium bound to glycine. High bioavailability. Glycine itself has independent sleep-promoting effects — published studies show glycine supplementation (3g) reduces sleep onset latency and improves subjective sleep quality. Taking glycinate means you get both effects in one supplement.

GI tolerance is excellent. Magnesium oxide and citrate are commonly associated with loose stools at effective doses. Glycinate rarely causes this.

### Magnesium L-Threonate — best if you want cognitive benefits too

Magnesium threonate was specifically developed for blood-brain barrier penetration. Rat studies showed 15% improvement in synaptic density in the hippocampus. Human studies suggest improved short-term memory and reduced anxiety.

For sleep specifically, the evidence is good but less robust than glycinate. The dose required is higher (1,500–2,000mg of the compound, delivering ~144mg elemental) and it's more expensive per milligram.

### What to avoid

**Magnesium Oxide** — Only 4% bioavailability. The most commonly sold form (look at the cheap supermarket bottles). Almost entirely useless for sleep or any other purpose. Avoid.

**Magnesium Citrate** — Decent bioavailability, but commonly causes loose stools at doses large enough to affect sleep. Fine if your stomach tolerates it.

## The dose that matters

The relevant figure is **elemental magnesium** — not the total weight of the compound. Check the label:

- 1,000mg of magnesium glycinate = approximately 140mg elemental magnesium
- 2,000mg of magnesium threonate = approximately 144mg elemental magnesium
- 500mg of magnesium oxide = approximately 300mg elemental magnesium (but only 4% absorbed = 12mg usable)

Target: **200–400mg elemental magnesium** from glycinate, bisglycinate, or threonate, taken 30–60 minutes before bed.
